Open and Closed Circuits

An open circuit is a circuit that does not form a complete conducting loop. Of course, by definition, it's not a circuit at all, as electric charge can't flow around a loop that is broken. If an open circuit is connected together to complete the circuit, then we have a closed circuit - that is, a circuit that forms a closed loop around which electric charge can flow. Most often, though, there's no need to refer to a circuit as "closed", as any working circuit is closed.

If you took the lightbulb circuit from , and cut a wire, you'll have created an open circuit, and current will stop flowing:

A short circuit is what happens when one part of a circuit is connected by a conductor unintentionally to another part of the circuit. Electric charge can then flow through a part of the circuit that it was not intended to. For example, in the battery and LED circuit in previous projects, attaching a wire from power to ground with nothing in between will cause large amounts of electricity to flow through that wire (instead of the LED as intended). This can dissipate your power source very quickly, or worst-case (for larger power sources than the battery we used), the flow of current can heat up the wire to the point where it breaks or causes a fire.

Pushing your buttons

The pushbutton (or just "button") is just a very simple switch – electric current can flow through it only when it is pressed.

Take one of the buttons out of your I/O (Input/Output) RaspberrySTEM Cell so we can take a closer look at how a button works. This is a representation of most basic pushbuttons – they have four “legs” that can be attached to your breadboard and wired to the circuit and then one button in the center of the device that can be pushed (“closed”) or released (“open”). When it is pushed closed, a small metal disk pushes into the legs of the button, connecting all four metal legs together.

Internally, the left side of the button (the two legs on the left) are connected together, and the right side of the button (the two legs on the right) are connected together:

The dashed lines above show the internal wires that permanently connect some of the legs together. So, when using a button, you'll generally want to just use two of the legs - either the top two, or the bottom two.

PIC: Animation of open/closed circuit (lightblub lighting), including sideways view of button.

When we insert a button into a circuit, it has one of two operations:

  1. When the button is left unpressed, it creates an open circuit, no electric current can flow through the button and nothing happens.

  2. When the button is pressed, the button acts as a wire and closes the circuit – electric current can then flow through the button.
