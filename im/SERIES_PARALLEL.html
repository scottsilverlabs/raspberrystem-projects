Going Loopy

All of our first circuits contained just one loop - from our power source through and LED and resistor, and then to the ground of our power source. In the real-world, circuits get more complicated as more components are added. Inside processors, there are millions upon millions of interconnected wires and components, making millions and millions of teensy loops.

Lets start with an example circuit we've seen before, a simple circuit that is a loop of two components: a battery and a resistor.

When you add a new component to the loop, assuming it has just two leads (legs) like a resistor does, there are exactly two ways you can do it. The first way is that you can add the component in series with the other component:

Series & Parallel Resistance

So what happens when you add a component is series or parallel? The water analogy works really well here to give us a picture of what's going on.

When resistances are in series, the total resistance adds up. That is, each resistance contributes more to resist the current around the circuit. For example, given one of our examples from above:

Battery/2-resistor series schematic, marked with R1 and R2

PIC: Battery/2-resistor WATER series schematic

So, in this case, the total resistance is the sum: R1 + R2

And, this means that: two equal resistances in series gives exactly twice the resistance.

When resistances are in parallel, the total conductance adds up. Conductance is the reciprocal of the resistance. In other words, resistance is a measure of how difficult it is for current to flow through a component, while conductance is the opposite - a measure of how easy it is for current to flow through a component. This is more evident in a picture:

Battery/2-resistor parallel schematic, marked with R1 and R2

PIC: Battery/2-resistor WATER parallel schematic

As you increase the number of resistors you allow more current to flow. So, in this case, the total conductance is the sum of the individual conductances, or: 1⁄R1 + 1⁄R2

The math for this can be a bit more confusing, but is very clear from the water analogy picture. Two equal resistors in parallel allow twice the amount of current to flow, or in other words, they resist the current flow less (half as much).

So, this means that: two equal resistances in parallel gives exactly half the resistance.
