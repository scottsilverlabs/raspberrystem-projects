What is Electricity?

Electricity is a foundation of our modern lives in the 21st century. With electricty, we heat and light our homes; power our electronic toys, games and gadgets; and run our appliances, tools, and equipment. But, perhaps most importantly, electricity enables us to use the microprocessor - the brain of all modern computers, including phones, laptops, tablets and more.

Simply stated, electricity involves the flow of charged particles (or more simply, a flow of "charge"). Charge is a physical property of matter. When charged particles are allowed to flow from one place to another, they transfer energy that can be used to power everything from a light bulb to an entire city. All of our everyday electronic gadgets – from cell phones to computers to microwave ovens – are powered by the flow of charge.

Electricity occurs naturally in everyday phenomemon such a lightning bolts and static shocks. But, only in the past few centuries, man has discovered how to convert differnet forms of energy to and from electrical energy. This feat allows us to transmit energy over large distances nearly instaneously, and has revolutionized the use of heat, light, and motors throughtout our homes, offices, and factories.

And, just in the past 100 years, we've taken another step in creating the mirocprocessor, which harnesses the power of electricity to control the flow of information. With the microprocessor, we can perform calculations a speeds that are not humanly possible...

What is a Circuit?

A circuit is a closed loop that allows charge to flow through it. The parts of a circuit that charge flows through are called conductors -- conductors are the backbone of circuits, and are generally made from metal wires. Conductors allow charge to flow through them and when you string together conductors in a closed loop, you have created an electric circuit. A circuit does not have to be just one single closed loop - they generally contain many intertwined loops.

A typical circuit has a power source, conductors, and other components. The power source forces charge to flow through the circuit. The conductors (wires) connect together the components in the circuit. And, the components are the parts of the circuit that are affected by the electric charge as it passes through them.

For example, the circuit above contains a battery and a lightbulb, attached together with wires. The battery serves as the power source, which forces charged particles through the wires. When the charge flows through the lightbulb, it illuminates.

Some circuits are very simple (like our battery and lightbulb example), while other circuits are very complicated, containing many power sources, wires and components.

The Water Analogy

Often, electricy can be a hard to get an intuitive understanding of because it is invisible. Charged particles flow through wires, but you can't see them. Because of this, it is often helpful to talk of the how the flow of charge through a conductor is very similar to the flow of water through a pipe.

This analogy isn't perfect, because the charged particles AREN'T water molecules, and in some ways behave differently. But there are many very strong similarities - often so similar that many of the equations we use to describe electric circuits are essentially the same equations used to describe the water flowing in pipes. Most of the electrical concepts throughout the Projects Guide use the water analogy to give a simple and intuitive understanding of what is going on inside a circuit. They are powerful and helpful, and can allow you to understand what's happening in a circuit without any equations. However, along the way we'll discuss the differences, too, so you can see where the analogy doesn't work.

Power Source

PIC: generic drawing or stock photo of a battery

Power sources provide the energy to make charge flow in circuits. There are many different types of power sources, and we'll talk more about them in .

In our simple circuit above, the power source is a battery. A battery converts chemical energy into electrical energy. That is, inside a battery, a specific chemical reaction occurs that causes charged particles to be pulled into one side of the battery, and pushed out the other.

In the water analogy, we model this as a pump - a device that pulls water into one side, and pushes out the other, analogous to a battery.

What's great about this analogy is it is intuitively obvious that exactly as much stuff (charge/water) goes into the power source (battery/pump) as comes out. The power source does not create charge just as a pump does not create water.

However, the water pump is just a model of the battery. The battery does not have any spinning moving parts inside. Both electrical batteries and water pumps are similar in that they convert one form of energy (chemical energy for the battery and mechanical energy for the pump) into another form of energy (electrical energy).

Another very common source of electrical power is a generator - and it does actually contain spinning moving parts - we'll discuss them more in .

Charge

PIC: generic drawing or stock photo of a atoms/electrons. Animated?

Atoms are the fundamental building blocks of all matter. Atoms themselves consist of 3 different particles: protons, neutrons and electrons. In the study of electricity, it is the protons and electrons that are of importance - they are the charged particles that make electricity possible.

Protons and electrons have an intrinsic property called charge. For a single proton and electron, the amount of charge is equal but opposite. Opposite charges are attracted to one another, while like charges repel each other. Protons have a positive charge, and electrons have an equal but opposite (negative) charge.

In ordinary matter, for example a metal conductor, the positive protons stay in a fixed location (the atom itself stays put), while some of the electrons are free to drift about the metal. It is these electrons that are responsible for the electric current.

Conductors

PIC: generic drawing or stock photo of wires

Conductors are the "pipes" of a circuit. Conductors are made of materials that allow charge to flow freely through them. In general, this means conductors are made out of metal, and copper is one of the most common metals used. Metals are special in that their structure has some "free" electrons that are not tightly bound to any specific atom. Instead, these electrons can flow freely throughout the metal, and are sometimes refered to as a "sea of electrons".

It's important to note that conductors are completely filled throughout with charge. Analogously, the pipes are fully filled with water. There is no air in the pipes.

There's some important differences between conductors and pipes that you should keep in mind:

  • Real pipes can leak water. Conductors cannot leak electrons - the electrons are held too tightly to the metal atoms to escape.

  • Metal conductors allow electrons to flow throughout the metal. Unlike a water pipe, there is no outside part (the pipe) that keeps the electrons in, they are held in by the strong pull of the protons in the metal atoms.

On terminology

Voltage, Current & Resistance

Voltage, Current & Resistance are three basic measurements that can be made in a circuit. We'll discuss these more in , but in the following projects, it will be useful to have a rough idea of what the are:

  • Voltage: The difference in charge between two points in a circuit.

  • Current: The flow of electric charge.

  • Resistance: The amount that a circuit component reduces the flow of charge.

Power & Ground

PIC: Lightbulb circuit with Power Ground and load marked.

In all circuits, we often talk about the following parts:

  • Power: This is generally the point of highest (most positive) charge in your circuit -- for example, the positive terminal of your battery. This point in a circuit is sometimes generically called "Vcc" or in the case of most of the projects we'll be doing, "3.3V" (since the power source we'll be using will be 3.3 volts). The power source provides the energy that makes the circuit do something useful.

  • Load: The part of the circuit that uses the flow of charge -- the components in the circuit -- is typically referred to as the Load. Above we talked about circuit resistance -- the Load is the part of the circuit that provides resistance. An example from our earlier circuit would be the lightbulb -- that is a part of the circuit Load. The Load uses the energy that the power source provides.

  • Ground: In an electric circuit, ground is generally the point of lowest (most negative) charge in the circuit. For example, in our lightbulb circuit above, the negative terminal of the battery is the point of lowest charge, and would be the physical ground point of this circuit. Interestingly, the reason we use the term "ground" is because in many electrical applications (like the electricity running through your house), the earth is used as both our reference point of lowest charge and our physical point of attaching the electrical circuit. This is because the earth is able to receive nearly infinite amounts of electricity without increasing its total charge, so we can always assume that the voltage between a power source and the earth will be constant.

Grounding Yourself

In our assembly guide, we mentioned that you should "ground" yourself before touching electronic components. Now that you have a conceptual idea of what ground is, we can explain this further. Have you ever gotten a static shock by touching someone or something? Sometimes, electric charge builds up in your body. This charge is then released -- in the form of a static discharge (or shock) when you touch someone or something that has a different amount of charge. In other words, if there is a voltage difference between you and some object, and you touch that object, you'll get a shock. Once your body has discharged the excess charge, you don't get any more shocks -- until, of course, you build up more charge.

If you were to touch an electrical component when you have a charge built up in your body, that resulting static shock that you feel could very well be enough to zap the component and break it (we sometimes call this "frying the component.") So, to avoid frying any electrical components, we recommend that you discharge any excess charge in your body before touching the components.

You can ground yourself by touching any piece of metal that is connected via some relatively direct path to the earth. Since all the electrical outlets in your house are connected to earth ground, you can generally ground yourself by touching the metal case of a electrical device that is plugged in. On the RaspberrySTEM CREATOR Kit, you can touch the metal casing on any of the USB ports or the Ethernet port on the back of the Raspberry Pi -- assuming the RaspberrySTEM CREATOR Kit is plugged into a wall outlet.
