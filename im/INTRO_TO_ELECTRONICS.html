What is Electricity

Need image or diagram, or cartoon

Simply stated, electricity is a flow of charged particles (electrons). When charged particles are allowed to flow from one place to another, they transfer energy that can be used to power everything from a light bulb to an entire city. All of our everyday electronic gadgets – from cell phones to computers to microwave ovens – are powered by the flow of these charged particles.

What is a Circuit

A circuit is a closed loop that allows charged particles to flow through it. The parts of a circuit that charged particles flow through are called conductors -- conductors are the building blocks of circuits, and metal wires are one example of a conductor. Wires and other conductors allow charged particles to flow through them and when you string together conductors in a closed loop, you have created an electric circuit.

A typical circuit has a power source, conductors, and various other components. The power source forces charged particles to flow through the circuit. The conductors (wires) connect together the components in the circuit. And, the components are the parts of the circuit that are affected by the electricity as it passes through them.

For example, a circuit may contain a battery and a lightbulb, attached together with wires. The battery serves as the power source, which forces charged particles through the wires. When the charged particles flow through the lightbulb, it illuminates.

The Water Analogy

Often, electricy can be a hard to get an intuitive understand of because it is invisible. Electrons (charged particles) flow through wires, but you can't see them. Because of this, it is often helpful to talk of the how the flow of electrons through a conductor is very similar to the flow of water through a pipe.

This analogy isn't perfect, because electrons AREN'T water. But there are many very strong similarities - often so similar that the equations we use to describe electricity are essentially the same equations used to describe the flow of water. Most of the electrical concepts throughout the Projects Guide use the water analogy to give a simple and intuitive understanding of what is going on inside a circuit. They are powerful and helpful, and can allow you to understand what's happining in a circuit without any equations. However, along the way we'll discuss the differences, too, so you can see where the analogy doesn't work.

Power Source

Power sources provide the energy to make electrons flow in circuits. There are many different types of power sources, and we'll talk more about them in .

In our simple circuit above, the power source is a battery. A battery converts chemical enery into electrical energy. That is, inside a battery, a specific chemical reaction occurs that causes electrons to be pulled into one side of the battery, and pushed out the other.

In the water analogy, we model this as a pump - a device that pulls in water from pulled into one side, and pushes out the other, similar to a battery.

What's great about this analogy is it is intuitively obvious that exactly as much stuff (electrons/water) goes into the power source (battery/pump) as comes out. The power source does not electrons and more than a pump creates water.

However, the water pump is just a model of the battery. The battery does not have any spinning moving parts inside. Both electrical batteries and water pumps are similar in that they convert one form of energy (chemical energy for the battery and mechanical energy for the pump) into another form of energy (electrical energy).

Still, another very common source of electrical power is a generator - and it does actually contain spinning moving parts - we'll discuss them more in .

Conductors

Conductors are the "pipes" of a circuit. Conductors are made of materials that allow electrons to flow freely through them. In general, this means conductors are made out of metal, and copper is one of the most common metals used. Metals are special in that their structure has some electrons that are not tightly bound to any specific atom. Instead, these electrons can flow freely throughout the metal, and are sometimes refered to as a "sea of electrons".

There's some important differences between conductors and pipes that you should keep in mind:

  • In the water analogy, all pipes is fully filled with water. There is no air in these pipes.

  • Real pipes can leak water. Conductors cannot leak electrons - the electrons are held too tightly to the metal atoms to escape.

  • Metal conductors allow electrons to flow throughout the metal. Unlike a water pipe, there is no outside part (the pipe) that keeps the electrons in, they are held in by the strong pull of the metal atoms.

Voltage

Voltage is essentially the difference in the amount of charge from one point in a circuit to another. An important concept of electricity is that charge will typically only flow between two points that have a difference in charge (a voltage). Specifically, charged particles will flow from a point of higher charge to a point of lower charge. If there is the same amount of charge at two points in a circuit, no electricity will flow.

Using our water analogy, voltage is analogous to the amount of pressure (basically, the force) that there is pushing water through a pipe filled with water. Imagine a water system that contains a water pump that is pushing water around a loop of a water-filled pipe (a 'circuit') -- water enters the pump at a low pressure and is ejected from the pump at a high pressure. It is this pressure difference that generates the flow of water. Likewise in an electric circuit, the electric power source is analogous to the water pump -- a power source takes in charge at a low voltage and pushes it out at a high voltage. It is this difference in voltage on the opposite sides of of the power source that generates the flow of charged particles.

Whenever there is a difference in voltages from one point to another in a circuit, and there is a conductive path for the electrons to flow between those two two points, the electrons will flow. This is true for forces in general - if you push someone else, they have to push back with the same amount of force, or else they will move. Current

Battery resistive circuit vs water resistive circuit

Current is the flow of electric charge through a circuit. Using our water analogy, current is the amount of water actually flowing through the pipe. In both cases, its measured by the amount of stuff (electrons or water) that flows through the pipe in an amount of time.

Its important to note that in our simple circuit example above, the current is exactly the same at every point in the circuit. That is, if your were to look at any small segment of the circuit, the same amount of electrons are going into that segment as are going out. This is true in the battery, the wires, and the resistor.

If you think about the water analogy, you might realize this must be true. If there was any segment of the circuit where there was more water going into that circuit than going out, then the pipe would ballon up at that point, or burst. But that doesn't happen - the flow of current happens around the entrie circuit all together, in lock step.

One thing that the water analogy doesn't give a good picture is how fast the elections actually move trhough the circuit. In a water circuit, you might imagine an actually water molecule moving, say, 1 foot along the pipe each second. In electrical circuits, the electrons move rought a million times slower.
