How electricity is made

Batteries play a critical part in powering our everyday devices, from mobile phones and laptops, to watches and hearing aids. Batteries use a chemical reaction to convert chemical energy into electrical energy.

However, most of the electrical energy we use everyday comes from the power company. This electrical energy is readily available through outlets around our homes, and heats our home, runs our computers, cooks our food, lights our rooms, recharges our batteries, and so much more... Where does the energy to do this come from, and how does it get to our homes?

There are a number of different ways that the power company provides electric power, but the most common way is through an electric generator. A generator is a device that converts mechanical energy (movement) into electrical energy. So, to generate electrical energy, all you need to do is provide the mechanical energy to turn the generator. For example, if you actually turn a generator by hand, then you can generate electrical energy.

One thing to remember, though - electrical energy is never "made" out of thin air. The energy already exists - we simply convert other forms of energy into electrical energy.

Funky magnets. How do they work?

It turns out that magnetism and electricity and are two tightly intertwined related concepts. Moving magnets can generate electric current in conductors, and likewise, electric currents can generate the same pulling forces that a magnet provides. When a magnet moves nears a closed loop of conducting wire, a current is generated in the loop. And the reverse is true as well: an electric current near a magnet will push the magnet.

Both of these concepts are used in two very similar items:

  • In an electric generator a loop of wire rotated around a magnet generates a current in that wire.

  • In an electric motor a current in a loop of wire causes a magnet to turn.

Electric motors are used everywhere - they spin washing machines, they run air conditioners and refrigerators, pump water, and even turn the wheels on electric vehicles.

The primary way to mechanically turn a generator is to push steam through a turbine (which is basically a large fan with many fan blades). The steam pushes the blades of the turbine, which turns the shaft of the generator to generate electrical energy. So how do you generate steam? You need to boil water, which you can do in many ways:

  • Burn something. This is the primary way generating electrical energy in the U.S. The main fuel we burn is coal, followed by natural gas.

  • Nuclear reactors use a process of "splitting" atoms which releases vasts amount of energy. This energy can be used to boil water.

  • Solar energy: Mirrors and lenses can reflect the sun's light to a small spot that geneartes huge amount of heat.

So that is how electrical energy is generated - but how does it go from being generated to powering the electronics in your home? The power company relies on a vast network of interconnected wires (often called "the grid") that deliver electrical energy to you and your neighbor's homes.

Boiling water to turn a turbine isn't the only way to turn a generator and generate electrical energy. In fact, sometimes you can turn generator directly. For example:

  • A hydroelectric power plant user the power of a water fall to turn the shaft of a generator.

  • A wind turbine uses the force of the wind to directly turn the shaft of a generator.

And, there are other ways to generate electrical energy as well, including solar cells, which directly convert the sun's energy to electric current, without any moving parts at all!
