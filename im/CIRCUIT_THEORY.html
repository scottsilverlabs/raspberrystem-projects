Voltage

PIC: Battery vs water circuits, with low/high voltage/pressure highlighted

Voltage is essentially the difference in the amount of charge from one point in a circuit to another. An important electrical concept is that charge will typically only flow between two points that have a difference in charge (a voltage). Specifically, charged particles will flow from a point of higher charge to a point of lower charge. If there is the same amount of charge at two points in a circuit, no electric charge will flow.

Using our water analogy, voltage is analogous to the amount of pressure (basically, the force) that there is pushing water through a pipe filled with water. Imagine a water system that contains a water pump that is pushing water around a loop of a water-filled pipe (a 'circuit') -- water enters the pump at a low pressure and is forced out of the pump at a high pressure. It is this pressure difference that generates the flow of water. Likewise in an electric circuit, the electric power source is analogous to the water pump -- a power source takes in charge at a low voltage and pushes it out at a high voltage. It is this difference in voltage on the opposite sides of of the power source that generates the flow of charged particles.

Whenever there is a difference in voltages from one point to another in a circuit, and there is a conductive path for the electrons to flow between those two two points, the electrons will flow. This is true for forces in general - if you push someone else, they have to push back with the same amount of force, or else they will move. The person who pushes with more force continues to move in the direction of that force.

Finally, voltage are (virtually) always talked about between two points. That is, we always discuss a voltage difference between those two points. For example, if you've every measured the voltage of a battery, you have to measure the voltage difference between the negative and positive sides. However, it is generally common to just discuss voltages at a single point in a circuit. This is because it is assumed that you are asking for the voltage difference between that point and ground, which is generally the lowest point of volatge in a circuit. We'll discuss ground more in later projects.

Current

PIC: Battery vs water circuits, with constant current highlighted

Current is the flow of electric charge through a circuit. Using our water analogy, current is the amount of water actually flowing through the pipe. In both cases, its measured by the amount of stuff (electrons or water) that flows through the pipe in an amount of time.

Its important to note that in our simple circuit example above, the current is exactly the same at every point in the circuit. That is, if your were to look at any small segment of the circuit, the same amount of electrons are going into that segment as are going out. This is true in the battery, the wires, and the resistor.

If you think about the water analogy, you might realize this must be true. If there was any segment of the circuit where there was more water going into that circuit than going out, then the pipe would ballon up at that point, or burst. But that doesn't happen - the flow of current happens around the entrie circuit all together, in lock step.

Resistance

PIC: Two water resistive circuits, with different resistances

If a water pipe is contricted (pinched), then less water can flow through it (for a certain water pressure). This concept is familiar to anyone who has played with a garden hose - if you pinch the hose, less water comes out. If you pinch the hose hard enough, no water comes out.

As you might imagine from the water analogy, if the more resistance there is the less current. That is, the more constricted the pipe is, the less amount of water that can flow through it.

Also notice that the voltage at the top of the resistor will be less than the voltage a the bottom of the resistor. This difference in voltage is called the voltage drop across the resistor. This is similar in the water analogy - when water is forced into a narrow pipe, the water gets "squished" together at the narrow entrance, and the pressure is greater there than after then end of the narrow region.
